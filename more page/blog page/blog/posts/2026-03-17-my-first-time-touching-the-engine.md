# Black Hand, Bleed Red: Mechanical Chemical

Today I changed my own oil for the first time. No one taught me. Just memory from watching the mechanic do it.

It did not go well. But it went.

First problem: couldn't find the right tool. Ended up using something that wasn't meant for the job, so I had to pry the drain bolt little by little instead of just spinning it off clean.

Then I stood there for way too long trying to figure out which direction to turn it. Righty tighty, lefty loosey — but when you're underneath a motor and your brain is second-guessing itself, nothing feels obvious.

When it finally came loose, warm used oil poured straight onto my hand. I remember the mechanic's hands always looked clean after this part. Mine were black, sticky, and warm. I don't know how he does it.

Waited for all the old oil to drain out. Then opened the new oil bottle. Then faced the next problem: the oil cap on this motor sits so close to the body there's almost no room to turn it.

I tried. Both directions. Couldn't get it. Tried harder. My hand got caught in the pliers and I actually got a small injury from that.

I gave up and asked the cashier to open it.

He did it in seconds.

I had been turning it the wrong way the whole time.

The embarrassing part is, while I was struggling with that cap, someone came in thinking I was the mechanic. They wanted to drop off their motor for a service. I had to tell them I wasn't. I felt bad. I probably have been working on this for too long, looked busy like I knew what I was doing from far away. Up close, not so much.

But fortunately, I didn't forget to close the drain bolt before pouring in the new oil. In the middle of all that mess, I remembered the one thing that actually mattered. If I'd forgotten that, the new oil would've gone straight to the floor and I'd have wasted the whole thing.

I Closed the bottom. Filled from the top. Started the motor.

It ran.

First time. No help. Mostly.
