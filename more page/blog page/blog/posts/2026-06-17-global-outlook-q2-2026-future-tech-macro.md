# The fog is coming; I can feel it before I can name it.

Something in the air between every headline, every keynote, every analyst notes I've been reading like scripture. Some say crisis is just the wheel doing one full 360. Maybe. But when you're standing underneath it, that doesn't make it lighter.

Two weeks ago, I watched on YouTube, Jensen Huang stood on a stage in San Jose and declared Physical AI the next big wave. Vera Rubin NVL72, $26 billion in open-weight models, robots toasting marshmallows by a campfire in an animated finale. I watched it like someone reads a weather report before a storm — technically informed, emotionally unprepared. The numbers were staggering. The implications were worse.

Everyone I follow was losing their mind over Google I/O. Gemini Omni, Gemini Spark, 650 million users, Samsung glasses dropping in fall. The timeline was electric. And I get it — I do. I scrolled through the same threads, watched the same clips, felt the same half-second of wonder. But somewhere between the third repost and the fourth "these changes everything," I just felt... tired. Not because it wasn't real. Because it was.

But the one that quietly haunted me the most — the one nobody in my timeline really stopped to sit with — was Majorana 2. Microsoft's quantum chip. It didn't get the hype of Gemini Omni or the memes of Jensen's robot campfire. It just... arrived. And if you know what quantum computing actually means for encryption, for drug discovery, for the entire architecture of how we secure and process information — you'd understand why I closed my laptop for a full five minutes after reading it. The others were upgrades. Majorana 2 is a different category of change. The kind that doesn't announce itself loudly. It just makes everything that came before it obsolete, eventually, quietly, on its own timeline.

That's when it hit me. Three months. Just three months and we got NVIDIA redefining what a chip can do, Microsoft turning agents into enterprise infrastructure, Google shipping the agentic future it had been teasing for years, Apple quietly drawing the line on who gets the good Siri and who doesn't. Oh, and the electrical grid is breaking. And SpaceX and Blue Origin want to put data centers in orbit. Three months. I've had situationships that lasted longer and made less impact.


And while all of this was happening in the server rooms and keynote stages, the world outside was doing its own thing. The Strait of Hormuz closed. Oil above $100. The IMF downgraded everything and Kristalina Georgieva went on a Bloomberg podcast, she's boomed and said,

_"I wake up worried that not yet people understand that we have to think of the unthinkable because the unthinkable has happened and it will happen again."_

I wrote that down. Stared at it for a while. Not because it was new information. Because someone official finally said it out loud.

So here I am. Analyst, reader, watcher. The grid is breaking, the rockets are pointed at orbit, the models are getting smarter, and somewhere out there she's building something I can't keep up with. The wheel keeps turning. 360 degrees, they say — crisis, recovery, crisis again. But nobody tells you what it feels like to be standing underneath it mid-rotation. Not at the bottom. Not at the top. Just... somewhere in the fog. Watching the keynotes. Reading the recaps. Taking notes on a world that is changing faster than I can feel it. The fog is still coming. I don't think it's going to clear anytime soon.
